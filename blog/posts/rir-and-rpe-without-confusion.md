RIR means reps in reserve: how many reps you could have done before failure. RPE is a related effort scale from 1 to 10. They are just ways to describe intensity.

Example: if a set feels like you had two good reps left, that is about 2 RIR, roughly RPE 8. If you had none left, that is 0 RIR, roughly RPE 10.

Why use these? Because your performance changes daily based on sleep, stress, nutrition, and life. Fixed percentages are useful, but autoregulation lets you adjust load while keeping effort in the right zone.

For most muscle-building work, 1 to 3 RIR is a great place to live. For heavy compound strength work, 2 to 4 RIR on most sets is often enough, with occasional harder top sets.

The big mistake is pretending you are at 2 RIR when you actually stopped at 5. Be honest. Your logbook does not care about optimism.

RIR and RPE are not complicated once you use them for a few weeks. They become a practical way to train hard and recover better.
