Compound lifts train multiple muscle groups at once. Think squat, bench, deadlift, row, overhead press. They are efficient, great for strength, and give you a lot of return per set.

Isolation exercises target one muscle more directly. Think leg extensions, hamstring curls, lateral raises, curls, tricep pushdowns. They are useful for bringing up weak points and adding volume with less systemic fatigue.

If you only do compounds, you might leave some muscles undertrained. If you only do isolation, you miss the heavy coordinated work that drives overall strength.

The sweet spot is simple: compounds first, isolations after. Get your main strength work done while fresh, then use accessory work to add focused hypertrophy.

Also, isolation movements are often safer places to push close to failure. That makes them great for high-effort work without the same injury risk as grinding heavy compounds.

Use both tools. Not because it sounds balanced, but because it is practical and effective.
